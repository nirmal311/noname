About Us

Suppose Joe wants to see charities benefit from his wealth now. With a charitable lead trust Joe would irrevocably place money or investment assets into a trust. Irrevocable means that once he makes the contribution, he can't take it back. Joe can then designate charities to receive income from the trust as it earns a return on investment. When Joe dies, what's left of the fund can be given to his family members or other beneficiaries such as the charities he was supporting.

There are variations of the charitable lead trust based on nuances in the tax structure. With a grantor trust, Joe would be able to take a full charitable deduction once he puts money in the trust. This has an immediate reduction on his taxes. With a non-grantor trust, the trust itself pays taxes and becomes eligible for its own charitable tax deductions. With the non-grantor trust type the tax benefit for Joe isn't that he reduces his income taxes each year. Instead his overall estate is reduced which can save his family the burden of having to deal with estate taxes.

To get even more specific, these funds can be further defined as unitrusts or annuity trusts. In a unitrust the trust pays out a fixed percentage of its value each year. With an annuity trust the trust pays out a specific amount of money rather than a calculated percentage. The unitrust structure benefits the people who receive money while Joe is alive since they can make more money if the trust rises in value. The annuity trust on the other hand benefits whoever receives the money after Joe's death since the fund's holdings stay in the account longer which translates into better growth potential over a long period of time.
